Wake Up and Smell the Cofree Comonads

2016-06-05

In the last post in this series, we talked about the rough sketch of an idea on how we might be able to make procedurally generated RPG stories. The general approach is this: make a super simple core set of story primitives, and then build more interesting abstractions on top of them.

Simplicity of our underlying language is desirable for a few reasons. The smaller our set of primitives, the easier a time we‚Äôre going to have proving things about what we can do with them. One thing we can do with them, particularly relevant to today‚Äôs discussion, is to provide an interpretation.

If you haven‚Äôt read the previous post in this series, now would probably be a good time.

Remember, the reason we wanted to build a DSL behind our story generation was so that we could use it to separate the structure of our story from its interpretation.

Last time, we used free monads over our command functor to generate our DSL. I promised today we‚Äôd use cofree comonads to interpret our language, but there is a lot of intermediate motivating material I want to get through before we discuss that. So without further ado, let‚Äôs talk about duality.

Dualityüîó

At first blush, duality can be understood as the mathematical version of bizarro world.

As a good rule of thumb, if I have some interesting mathematical object XXX, then its dual, co-XXX, is the opposite mathematical object, and is also interesting.

But what does opposite mean, here?

I‚Äôm by no means a mathematician (yet!), but, to a (very) rough approximation, a dual is constructed by flipping all of your arrows backwards. What arrows, you might ask? Well, that‚Äôs a good question. Let‚Äôs look at an example. It‚Äôll involve drawing pretty pictures, so make sure you have your copy book ready.

Products and coproductsüîó

Okay, so given a‚ààAa \in Aa‚ààA and b‚ààBb \in Bb‚ààB, we have (a,b)‚ààA√óB(a, b)\in A \times B(a,b)‚ààA√óB, and we call this (a,b)(a, b)(a,b) a (cartesian) product. Intuitively, a product is pair of two things, wrapped up together in a nice little package. The words ‚Äúproduct‚Äù and ‚Äúpair‚Äù are interchangeable, so go wild with it!

More formally, we can encode the idea of a product thusly:

prod :: (a -> b) -> (a -> c) -> a -> (b, c)
prod f g a = (f a, g a)

Which is to say, given two functions, a -> b and a -> c, we can create a new function which maps a -> (b, c). The fact that prod is polymorphic in all a, b, c should be telling that we‚Äôre onto something here.

Let‚Äôs dive in a little further, and investigate this notion as a commutative diagram, because the idea of duality is a little easier to investigate in that context. Our prod function above can also be encoded equivalently by this diagram:

\begin{xy} \xymatrix { A \ar@/_/[ddr]_f \ar@{.>}[dr]|{prod} \ar@/^/[drr]^g \\ & B \times C \ar[d]^{fst} \ar[r]_{snd} & C \\ & B } \end{xy}

If you view the capital letters as types and the arrows as functions, this corresponds perfectly with our product function as written above. The solid arrows are ones we know that exist, and the dashed line is our proposition: ‚Äúif everything else in this picture holds, this arrow exists.‚Äù

So: the million dollar question. What happens when we flip all of our arrows around? We get this diagram:

\begin{xy} \xymatrix { & C \ar@/^/[ddr]^g \ar[d] \\ B \ar[r] \ar@/_/[drr]_f & ? \ar@{.>}[dr]|{coprod} \\ & & A } \end{xy}

Which of course corresponds with this in Haskell:

coprod :: (b -> a) -> (c -> a) -> Coproduct b c -> a

You probably know what this is, but let‚Äôs pretend like we don‚Äôt, and see if Hoogle can answer this for us. Spoilers, it can. That‚Äôs right! It‚Äôs our old friend either!

either :: (b -> a) -> (c -> a) -> Either b c -> a

Cool! So a coproduct is a sum type, and is the dual to the product type. For the sake of completeness, let‚Äôs fill in all of the missing labels on our diagram.

\begin{xy} \xymatrix { & C \ar@/^/[ddr]^g \ar[d]_{Right} \\ B \ar[r]^{Left} \ar@/_/[drr]_f & B+C \ar@{.>}[dr]|{either} \\ & & A } \end{xy}

Notice that our interesting product type had an interesting dual. This is theme we will continuously take advantage of.

It is left as an exercise to the reader to prove that the dual of the coproduct is the product (this is not a very hard proof since arrows only have two ends).

Monads and comonadsüîó

Lovely. Armed with our new superpower of duality, we‚Äôre now ready to take on comonads. Judging from the name, we should expect them to be dual to monads. Recall that a monad m is defined by two functions:

  • return :: a -> m a
  • (>>=) :: m a -> (a -> m b) -> m b

Let‚Äôs flip the arrows around, and since we‚Äôre flipping everything else, we‚Äôll refer to our comonad as w, which is defined by two functions dual to the monad‚Äôs:

  • extract :: w a -> a
  • extend :: w b -> (w b -> a) -> w a

The full intuition behind comonads is left as an exercise to the reader (my monad tutorial didn‚Äôt go too well), but a good starting point is this: while monads are for building up a computation in a context, comonads compute values from a context.

The canonical example of a comonad is Conway‚Äôs game of life (a cell is alive or dead based on how lively its neighborhood is.) Another particularly amazing example is spreadsheets (the value of a cell depends on the value of other cells it references.)

Wake up and smell the cofreeüîó

Recall the definition of the free monad:

data Free f a = Pure a
              | Bind (f (Free f a))

Now that I‚Äôve primed you, it should be pretty clear that this is a sum type ‚Äì Free f a is either a Pure a or a Bind (f (Free f a)). There are no function arrows to flip around, so we can dualize this trivially now that we know products and coproducts are duals of one another:

data Cofree f a = Cofree a (f (Cofree f a))

Again, it‚Äôs hard to get a sense of what this might mean just by looking at it. Let‚Äôs throw some concrete functors at it:

data Cofree Maybe a = Cofree a (Maybe (Cofree Maybe a))

Whoa! Look at that! Cofree Maybe a is at least one a, followed by maybe more. That‚Äôs just a non-null list in disguise! Veeeery interesting, no? I wonder what happens if we slap in the list functor instead:

data Cofree [] a = Cofree a [Cofree [] a]

Hey, this one is equivalent to a rose tree ‚Äì an n-ary tree with data at every branch.

We must be onto something here ‚Äì those are pretty different data structures, and we got them just by changing the functor underlying our Cofree.

As you might expect, Cofree is thusly named because it generates trivial comonads for free (as in time) given a functor f:

instance Functor f => Comonad (Cofree f) where
    extract   (Cofree a _ )   = a
    extend wb@(Cofree _ bc) f = Cofree (f wb) (fmap (`extend` f) bc)

It‚Äôs probably the dumbest comonad instance imaginable ‚Äì there is no context to extract values from, we just pull out the a we have. But again, it‚Äôs good that our instance is stupid. That‚Äôs what we want ‚Äì that‚Äôs why we made it.

Unfortunately it‚Äôs a little harder for us to bask in the glory of having a cofree comonad ‚Äì comonads don‚Äôt give rise to unique syntax in Haskell, so we‚Äôll just have to be content with the fact that our instance compiles.

This feels like a natural place to end off, so we will. Next time around we‚Äôll take a look at adjunctions, how they give rise to pairings between functors, and how we can use that machinery to automatically pair our cofree comonads with our free monads into one mega DSL-implementing wonder device.

Until then!
